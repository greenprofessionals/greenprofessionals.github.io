Strategic Intelligence Brief

APC Party Profile: Origins, Power Machine, 2028 Succession, and Fault Lines

A structured, evidence-based intelligence brief for SLPP strategists on how the All People's Congress is built, who is contesting its leadership, and where its internal divisions create openings ahead of 2028.

Fomba Kassoh · Secretary General, SLPP North America (NY Chapter) · Executive Secretary for Strategy & Political Affairs, Green Professionals · May 2026, updated July 2026 · 102 pages

The All People's Congress is the only opposition force in Sierra Leone capable of forming a government on its own. Any SLPP strategy that treats it as a static, predictable rival underestimates its capacity for reinvention. This brief gives SLPP planners, communicators, and field organizers a structured, evidence-based understanding of how APC is built, who is contesting its leadership, where its internal divisions sit, and how those features create both threats and openings for SLPP between now and 2028.

Editor's Update · July 2026

Three developments since original publication materially affect this brief's succession analysis. First, and most significant: APC National Chairman Alhaji Minkailu Mansaray died on 15 June 2026 in Dakar, Senegal, at age 75, three days after his birthday, following a prolonged illness that had kept him in London and later Senegal for medical treatment. Deputy Chairman Ambassador Osman Foday Yansaneh, who had been serving as Acting Chairman during Mansaray's extended absence, now leads the party into the August convention without the man widely regarded as the APC's most institutionally knowledgeable strategist and a key power broker in delegate politics. President Bio, Vice President Jalloh, and former President Koroma all issued condolences, an unusual moment of cross-party unity. Second, the National Delegates Conference has been formally confirmed for 9–12 August 2026 at Bai Bureh Hall, Port Loko Township — the internal elections calendar (ward, constituency, district, regional, and diaspora conventions) ran from February through April 2026 and has now concluded, meaning delegate composition for the August NDC is largely locked in. Third, the Sam-Sumana eligibility dispute escalated: the Political Parties Regulation Commission ordered APC to regularize his membership status after finding the party bypassed its own constitutional disciplinary procedures when it declared his membership forfeited, a ruling that strengthens his legal position but has not resolved APC's internal resistance to his candidacy. See the updated analysis embedded in Chapters 4, 5, and 9 below.

Chapter 2
Strategic Brief — Implications for SLPP

APC is at once a popular machine, a northern–western electoral fortress, and a political institution permanently shadowed by its own history. SLPP planning that ignores either the machine strength or the historical baggage will be wrong in both directions.

The All People's Congress, founded in October 1960, still lives with a double inheritance. In its own constitutional self-telling, APC was created to fight class, tribal, and social exclusion and to build a mass-based democratic movement. In the historical and academic record, however, the party is equally inseparable from the Siaka Stevens era of patronage, centralization, and one-party rule. That legacy was later linked by scholars and Sierra Leone's Truth and Reconciliation Commission to the institutional decay that helped create the conditions for war. For SLPP messaging, this means APC can always be reminded of its past; for SLPP organizers, it means APC's mobilizing reflexes are deeper than its current opposition status suggests.

APC's contemporary power is distributed across two parallel systems. The formal system is constitutional: delegates elect the flagbearer at a National Delegates Conference. The informal system is political: former President Ernest Bai Koroma, currently in Nigeria under a 2024 ECOWAS-mediated arrangement after being charged with treason in January 2024, still matters symbolically and organizationally.

The Central Finding

None of the current APC aspirants alone yet controls all four APC needs: cash, structure, crossover geography, and external legitimacy. That gap is SLPP's most important strategic opening.

The succession battle is real but the official list remains fluid. The strongest documentary-grade source on the field is the Institute for Governance Reform's December 2025 report, which said APC leaders had not formally recognized any aspirants at the time, even though media review yielded a 16-name long list. On that evidence, Sheikh Mohamed "Jagaban" Kamara was the only APC figure above 50 percent electability nationally, while Samura Kamara remained the next most favorable major name, followed by Sam Sumana and Chericoco. Public declarations have since widened the field further to include Yvonne Aki-Sawyerr, Ady Macauley, Richard Konteh, Osman Timbo, Joseph Fitzgerald Kamara, Bai Mahmoud Bangura, Kaifala Marah, Alhassan Gbassay Kanu, Suphian Kalokoh, Ibrahim Bangura, Sorie Sulaiman Sesay, and others.

The Four-Task Test — Both Sides

APC's actual 2028 task is fourfold: hold the northern and north-western heartland, preserve or grow its edge in Western Area, open at least one breach in the East through Kono or urban crossover appeal, and reassure international actors that an APC government would be governable, constitutional, and non-disruptive. SLPP's counter-task is symmetrical: protect the South-East, contest Western Area, deny APC the East, and keep the international community calm enough that it does not feel obliged to pressure for power-sharing concessions.

Three SLPP Imperatives

Three SLPP imperatives flow from this brief. First, do not let APC unify before SLPP does. Second, contest Western Area as if it were SLPP's most important district, because it is. Third, sustain the Tripartite reform engagement even when it is uncomfortable; the international moral guarantors are SLPP's most cost-effective insurance policy against another 2023-style legitimacy crisis.

Chapter 3
Origins, Ideology, and the Long Inheritance

APC's official constitutional narrative says the party was founded on 17 October 1960 to create a society in which no class, tribe, or creed would dominate another. Historically, Siaka Stevens emerged from a split in the late-colonial nationalist camp, first through the Elections Before Independence Movement and then the APC, which became the main opposition to the SLPP after independence. The party grew out of dissatisfaction with the post-colonial settlement and became a vehicle for populist anti-SLPP mobilization.

What Stevens built, however, was not only an opposition party. Academic work and TRC-derived analysis portray APC's long rule as a patron-client system that weakened formal institutions, leaned heavily on recruitment through patronage, and progressively displaced public governance into informal networks. The 1978 one-party constitution, ratified by a heavily manipulated plebiscite, formalized APC as the sole legal party until 1991.

One important scholarly argument, however, is that APC's original strength lay not simply in northern ethnic sentiment, but in its ability to mobilize unemployed youth, traders, and constituencies alienated by both elite politics and chiefdom structures. That helps explain a lasting APC trait. Even when it speaks the language of constitutionalism, it often behaves like a movement that trusts mobilization more than procedure.

Reinvention after 2018

The post-2018 APC reinvented itself institutionally after losing state power. Its 2022 constitution codified a more elaborate structure, recognized diaspora branches as a full region of the party, formalized the Elders Council, Women's Congress, Young Congress, and internal electoral bodies, and set the rule that a presidential candidate is elected by delegates and must secure 50 percent plus one. It also requires that flagbearer elections be held at least two years before the presidential election when APC is in opposition. That rule is central to why the August 2026 National Delegates Conference matters so much for 2028.

The 2023 Crisis and the Agreement for National Unity

The October 2023 Agreement for National Unity committed APC to end non-participation in governance and created the Tripartite Committee on electoral systems and management bodies review. ECOWAS, AU, UNOWAS, and the Commonwealth invested diplomatic capital in stabilizing the impasse. As of the April 2026 third joint assessment mission, that monitoring is still active and has produced a fresh roadmap requiring a comprehensive restructuring of the Electoral Commission for Sierra Leone by 31 December 2026.

The Koroma Factor and the November 2023 Incident

Former President Ernest Bai Koroma remains the most important informal figure inside APC even when he is not physically present. On 26 November 2023, gunmen attacked the central military armory at Wilberforce Barracks in Freetown and broke into Pademba Road prison, releasing more than a thousand inmates; about 20 people were killed. The Bio government described the event as an attempted coup. In January 2024 Koroma was charged with four offenses including treason and misprision of treason; the government said the alleged plot involved members of his security detail. After ECOWAS intervention led by President Tinubu, Koroma was permitted to travel to Nigeria on medical grounds, where he has remained.

SLPP Guidance on the Koroma Case

The Koroma case is double-edged. It removed the APC's most experienced unifying figure from day-to-day politics and made it harder for the party to coordinate a single succession behind closed doors. It also gave APC a powerful internal grievance narrative that ties the party closer together, especially in Bombali, Karene, and parts of Port Loko. SLPP communicators should treat the case as a legal matter only and avoid public commentary that hands APC additional mobilization material.

Chapter 4
Organizational Architecture and Power Structure

Formally, APC is a delegate-driven party with a dense vertical structure. The constitution establishes a National Delegates Conference, National Advisory Committee, National Executive Committee, Elders Council, Women's Congress, Veterans Congress, Young Congress, district and constituency bodies, polling center executives, diaspora branches, and independent internal elections and complaints mechanisms. The Elders Council is explicitly called the conscience of the Party and may mediate disputes, advise the party, and help raise funds. The diaspora is not treated as a side network but as an actual APC region.

Who Really Decides?

The formal selectorate matters, but so do perceptions of who really decides. IGR's nationwide December 2025 study found that 70 percent of self-identified APC and SLPP supporters believed delegates choose the flagbearer; yet the same report also recorded widespread concern that national officials and big men can shape delegate choices informally. Even more significant, 74 percent of APC supporters preferred a direct primary where all party card holders vote, rather than leaving the decision to a small class of senior figures.

Tension Inside the Party

APC's constitutional order is delegate-based, but a large share of its grassroots would prefer a more plebiscitary system. That contradiction is one of the most important fault lines in the entire APC story — and one SLPP should watch but never publicly inflame.

Update · The Chairmanship Vacancy

The death of National Chairman Minkailu Mansaray on 15 June 2026 removes the figure most credited with holding APC's formal and informal systems together. Mansaray had chaired the party since his 1,354-vote landslide election at the February 2023 convention and was widely regarded, including by rival SLPP figures, as the APC's most institutionally fluent strategist. Deputy Chairman Osman Foday Yansaneh, who had already been serving as Acting Chairman during Mansaray's prolonged medical absence in London and Senegal, now leads the party through the final stretch before the August convention. SLPP planners should treat the chairmanship itself, not just the flagbearer race, as contested terrain at the NDC — a chairmanship transition creates additional uncertainty in an already crowded succession picture.

Digital Modernization

APC's digital modernization is politically significant. The official APC platform markets the MEMS system as a member-management, finance, and campaign-organization tool. It advertises financial transparency, documentation, and campaign data organization. It hosts different payment channels for Sierra Leone, diaspora communities, and patron members, and promotes a turnout-monitoring viewer for lower-level elections. This means APC is trying to become simultaneously more data-driven and more subscription-based without giving up patronage politics.

Funding Architecture

Article 66 of the APC constitution lists registration fees, subscriptions, diaspora donations, contributions from qualified voters, mandatory monthly contributions from elected and appointed officials who are party members, fundraising proceeds, investment returns, and fines as party funding sources. On paper, APC is much more institutionalized than the common public image of a merely personality-driven machine would suggest.

The problem is Sierra Leone's wider campaign-finance environment. The EU Election Observation Mission found that campaign finance in 2023 was inadequately regulated, with no fair ceilings on income or spending, no meaningful interim reporting, no requirement for disclosure of donations or expenses, and weak enforcement by the Political Parties Regulation Commission. That means the question of who funds APC cannot be answered with audited certainty; it can only be answered through visible channels and probable network analysis.

Mobilization Spine

APC's mobilization spine is still local and human. In 2023, observers found campaigning remained highly personality-centered and heavily dependent on tours, door-to-door canvassing, and offline amplification of digital messages. That combination remains APC's street advantage: it can fuse local ward structures, ataya-base conversations, youth operators, radio, WhatsApp circulation, and district-level elites into fast mobilization. Its weakness is the same thing. Once mobilization outruns discipline, the party becomes vulnerable to factional fights, rumor cascades, and violent escalation.

Chapter 5
The 2026 Convention and Candidate Profiles

APC had not officially recognized any aspirant at the time of IGR's December 2025 survey, even though campaigning was already underway. This profile distinguishes between publicly declared candidates, prominent rumored candidates, and under-documented names. Each candidate is presented as an APC delegate would recognize him or her, with vulnerabilities noted so SLPP communicators can plan against the strongest version of each opponent. The succession field is crowded. What follows is one card per candidate. SLPP planners should use these profiles to anticipate, not to publish.

5.1 Sheikh Mohamed Omodu Kamara — "Jagaban"
Public Status
Active de facto aspirant; heavily campaigned and polled
Strongest Base
Western Area, Northwest, business-philanthropy networks, visible East roots
APC Selling Point
Money, excitement, crossover public favorability
Primary Vulnerability
Money-politics critique; thin governing track record

Jagaban is the standout story of the current APC race because he is the only figure in IGR's December 2025 poll above 50 percent electability nationally. IGR recorded especially strong numbers for him in Western Area and the Northwest, while his biography and media profile also give him an eastern story unusual for APC aspirants: he is publicly identified with Blama in the East, but he has built a national identity around philanthropy, entrepreneurship, and splashy political giving. His weaknesses are equally clear. His public political record is thinner than those of Kaifala, Samura, Richard, JFK, or Sam Sumana. Critics are already framing his rise as excessively money-driven, which may be exaggerated but captures a real perception risk.

5.2 Dr. Samura Kamara
Public Status
Uncertain or ambiguous; still powerful but signaling hesitation
Strongest Base
North and Northwest establishment plus 2023 residual machine
APC Selling Point
Recognition, old structure, diplomatic credibility
Primary Vulnerability
Age, charisma fatigue, old-case baggage, defeat fatigue

Samura remains the most consequential non-Jagaban figure because his machine is residual, not hypothetical. He was APC's presidential candidate in both 2018 and 2023, previously served as central bank governor, finance minister, and foreign minister, and still polls as the party's second-most favorable major name among self-identified supporters. But Samura's liabilities are mounting. Two defeats create obvious fatigue. On the electoral map, he remains weak in the South and East. Current reporting suggests he is publicly distancing himself from an immediate 2028 drive.

5.3 Yvonne Aki-Sawyerr
Public Status
Publicly declared
Strongest Base
Freetown, urban professionals, women, diaspora
APC Selling Point
Urban governance, reform image, global legitimacy
Primary Vulnerability
Rural delegate depth and old-guard suspicion

Yvonne Aki-Sawyerr is APC's clearest urban-modernizer option. She brings a profile unlike any other serious contender: two terms as Mayor of Freetown, a global city leadership network, a professional finance background, and a public brand built around data-driven governance, transparency, and climate-resilience urban reform. Her attractiveness is strongest in Western Area, among women, younger urban professionals, diaspora donors, and voters tired of old APC male hierarchies. She also lacks the obvious backward link into APC's northern patronage estate. Her biggest campaign problem would not be foreign diplomacy; it would be internal anthropology.

5.4 Dr. Kaifala Marah
Public Status
Public aspirant
Strongest Base
Kono–Koinadugu bridge, technocrats, investors
APC Selling Point
Strong economic competence narrative
Primary Vulnerability
Old-establishment baggage, weaker street charisma

Kaifala Marah is APC's most conventionally technocratic economic aspirant. He has served as State House chief of staff, finance minister, and governor of the Bank of Sierra Leone. Biographically, he is publicly linked to Koidu in Kono and to Koinadugu and Kuranko roots, giving him a natural bridge between East and North that few APC contenders can claim. His strengths are competence, elite reassurance, and possible investor confidence.

5.5 Chernor Maju Bah — "Chericoco"
Public Status
Public aspirant and Leader of the Opposition
Strongest Base
Parliamentary colleagues, Western Area, APC's formal opposition machine
APC Selling Point
Parliamentary gravitas, institutional knowledge, unity candidate potential
Primary Vulnerability
High insider-profile, lower public excitement; tied to previous ticket

Chericoco is the most plausible unity-parliamentary compromise candidate. His strengths are institutional: he knows the structures, has allies across the party, and carries the dignity of the formal opposition role. His challenge is that the same institutional identity gives him little fuel for populist excitement, and his association with the Samura Kamara ticket in previous cycles is a weight rather than a lift in a field where renewal is at a premium.

5.6 Alhaji Samuel Sam-Sumana
Public Status
Declared; returned to APC September 2025
Strongest Base
Kono, Eastern Province, former VP network
APC Selling Point
Eastern crossover capacity, VP-level name recognition
Primary Vulnerability
2015 expulsion, party-trust deficit, ECOWAS vs Supreme Court tension, contested flagbearer eligibility

Sam Sumana is the most obvious eastern and Kono disruptor in the field. He returned to APC in September 2025 after running under the C4C ticket in 2018. His value is geographical: Kono is the Eastern Province's most electorally flexible district, and Sumana's capacity to consolidate it for APC would be significant. His liability is the unresolved tension between the ECOWAS Court's ruling in his favor and the Sierra Leone Supreme Court's ruling against him, plus the organizational skepticism his long absence from the party has created.

Update, 2026: APC's Secretary-General moved to disqualify Sam-Sumana from the 2028 flagbearer contest, citing his prior expulsion and readmission terms. Sam-Sumana filed a formal five-page complaint with the Political Parties Regulation Commission, which subsequently ruled that APC had bypassed its own constitutional disciplinary procedures — no hearing before the Disciplinary and Grievance Committee, no referral to the NDC — in declaring his membership forfeited, and ordered the party to regularize his status. Kono-district APC figures have warned the district could reject the party entirely in 2028 if his exclusion stands. The PPRC ruling strengthens Sam-Sumana's legal position but does not compel APC to place him on the August convention ballot; SLPP should treat his eligibility as unresolved heading into the NDC, with a real chance the dispute becomes a live legitimacy question inside APC's own convention.

5.7–5.15 Additional Declared Aspirants

The full field also includes Ady Macauley (former ACC Commissioner; declared December 2025; technocratic reform lane), Richard Konteh (twice-cleared former Chief of Staff; D-UNIFIER branding; 88 convention votes in 2023), Osman Timbo (Deputy Secretary-General; Western Area MP; helped draft 2022 APC Constitution), Joseph Fitzgerald Kamara (former ACC Commissioner, AG; institutional gravitas; 2018 bid blocked on membership grounds), Bai Mahmoud Bangura (youth wing representation), Alhassan Gbassay Kanu (recent defection back to APC; Freetown and diaspora network), Suphian Kalokoh (Northern aspirant), Ibrahim Bangura (academic and peace-sector profile; Oxford fellowship; DIB Movement), Sorie Sulaiman Sesay (emerging Northern figure), and others whose public declarations have been reported without sufficient profile information for full assessment at this time.

Chapter 6
Electoral Geography and the National Map

Sierra Leone's electoral map is structurally stable but not static. APC's core territory runs through the Northern and North-Western Provinces — Bombali, Tonkolili, Kambia, Port Loko, Falaba, Karene. SLPP's core territory runs through the Southern and Eastern Provinces. Western Area is the contested zone that has determined the last two presidential elections. Identity and the Two-Party Logic

Sierra Leone remains a de facto two-party system in which political identity is still heavily recruited through ethnic and regional affinities rather than ideology. International IDEA's 2025 democracy assessment says as much directly. Even democratic breakthroughs did not dissolve ethnic cleavages so much as rework them through urban heterogeneity and strategic alliances. The cleanest internal formulation for SLPP planners is this: APC is not an ethnic party, but it is a party that cannot escape ethnic and regional readings. SLPP is in a structurally similar position. Both parties have to manage the same fact while saying the opposite about themselves in public.

Chapter 7
Internal Fault Lines — SLPP Wedge Points

APC is not a single coalition. It is at least five overlapping ones, each pulling in slightly different directions. SLPP's most cost-effective messaging is the messaging that does not invent attacks but instead amplifies divisions APC is already arguing about with itself.

Wedge Point 1 · Generational
What APC Argues About
Convention rules, primary vs delegate vote, vetting fees, age implicit in 'experience' criteria.
SLPP Messaging Hook
Highlight that SLPP has visibly placed younger Cabinet faces; contrast quietly with APC's protracted elder-vs-youth fight.
What Not to Do
Do not name specific APC youth leaders favorably; it accidentally elevates them.
Wedge Point 2 · Geographic
What APC Argues About
Whether the next flagbearer should consolidate the northern base or contest Western Area; whether to pick a northerner with an urban running mate, or vice versa.
SLPP Messaging Hook
Western Area is genuinely contestable; concentrate field, surrogate, and media spend on Freetown wards rather than chasing the impossible North.
What Not to Do
Do not reinforce the North-vs-Mende frame; SLPP wins when the framing is competence, not geography.
Wedge Point 3 · Financial
What APC Argues About
Whether Jagaban's financial dominance represents legitimate democratic energy or the purchase of a nomination.
SLPP Messaging Hook
Let APC argue this in public. Do not attach SLPP's name to any faction in the money fight.
What Not to Do
Do not attack Jagaban on money grounds directly; it sounds like fear of a popular candidate.
Wedge Point 4 · Ideological / Legacy
What APC Argues About
Whether the 2023 electoral defeat was a legitimate loss or a stolen result; how far to cooperate with the Tripartite process.
SLPP Messaging Hook
Keep delivering on Tripartite commitments. The fraction of APC that wants reform will be silently reassured; the fraction that wants confrontation will be isolated.
What Not to Do
Do not relitigate 2023 in public. SLPP wins when the frame is the future, not the past.
Wedge Point 5 · The Koroma Question
What APC Argues About
Whether aspirants should seek Koroma's blessing, distance themselves from his legal situation, or chart fully independent courses.
SLPP Messaging Hook
Let the legal process run. Do not gloat; do not comment. Every APC argument about Koroma is a day APC is not talking about SLPP governance.
What Not to Do
Do not make the legal case a political trophy. Martyrdom narratives in Bombali and Karene are real.

Chapter 8
External Posture — Reform Engagement, Diaspora, Koroma

Internationally, APC is both necessary and watched. ECOWAS, AU, UNOWAS, and the Commonwealth mediated the 2023 settlement because APC is too central to Sierra Leonean democratic legitimacy to be allowed to remain outside the system. The same partners have been explicit about concerns over transparency, disputed results, intimidation, and the broader constitutional environment.

The Carter Center said it did not have confidence that the 2023 results accurately reflected the will of the people, while the EU mission described the tabulation process as opaque and statistically inconsistent. SLPP cannot wish that critique away; it can only manage it. The Tripartite framework is the management tool, and continued visible cooperation with it is SLPP's lowest-cost form of international insurance.

The Diaspora Architecture

APC's diaspora architecture is formally embedded in its constitution. Diaspora branches are recognized as a full APC region. In practice, diaspora networks in the United States, United Kingdom, and Europe provide both financial flows and media amplification capacity. A significant portion of APC's most energetic online defenders operate from diaspora communities. SLPP should treat diaspora engagement not as a cosmetic exercise but as a genuine front in the 2028 contest. Diaspora voices that can articulate a credible, forward-looking SLPP platform in English, using international democratic norms as their frame, are among SLPP's most cost-effective communicators.

The External Question — Both Sides

The international community is not asking whether APC will oppose SLPP. It is asking whether APC can oppose responsibly, and whether SLPP can hold power responsibly enough that a clean 2028 election is possible. Both halves of that question are SLPP's to answer.

Chapter 9
Strategic Implications and SLPP Response Matrix

Do not let APC unify before SLPP does. Contest Western Area as if it were SLPP's most important district, because it is. Sustain the Tripartite reform engagement even when it is uncomfortable.

If Jagaban Wins the APC Nomination

A Jagaban candidacy creates a specific challenge: he is the one APC aspirant currently capable of crossing the 50 percent national electability line in polling. SLPP's counter is not to attack his money — that reads defensively — but to contest him on the governance gap. A man who has never run a ministry, a city, a national agency, or any public institution of consequence should not be trusted to run a country. Frame the contest as experience and delivery versus excitement and philanthropy. Western Area, where Jagaban is strongest, should be the primary investment zone for SLPP between now and 2028.

If a Technocrat Wins (Marah, Konteh, JFK, Macauley)

A technocratic APC candidate is SLPP's most intellectually demanding opponent because the debate shifts to policy competence rather than character or money. SLPP's response should be to out-credential on delivery: what has Bio's government actually built, fixed, digitized, or improved? Each ministry should be prepared with a 90-second evidence-based delivery answer. The technocratic lane is also APC's most internationally palatable option, which means SLPP should be prepared for a warmer external reception for whichever technocrat emerges.

If a Unity or Compromise Candidate Emerges

A compromise candidacy — most likely Chericoco in a brokered outcome — would signal that APC's factions could not agree on a first-choice candidate. That is a soft signal of internal incoherence that SLPP should note but not amplify publicly. A weakened APC that coalesces around a second-choice candidate can be more electorally dangerous than a first-choice candidate who alienates a faction. SLPP should plan for both an energized APC and a resentful-but-disciplined one.

The Chairmanship Vacancy as a New Variable

Mansaray's death adds a second contested race inside the same August convention: the chairmanship itself. Historically the APC chairmanship and the flagbearer role have been held by different individuals who then had to co-govern the party; a chairmanship vacuum at the same convention that selects a flagbearer increases the odds of factional horse-trading, slate-building, and last-minute alliance formation. SLPP should not expect APC's succession to be a single clean contest. It is now, structurally, two overlapping contests happening in the same hall on the same dates — a dynamic that increases both the odds of visible APC disunity and the odds of an emergency unity push that SLPP should watch for closely in the weeks before 9 August.

The SLPP Four-Track Preparation Programme

From this brief, four tracks of SLPP preparation emerge. Track one is candidate intelligence: continue monitoring aspirant declarations, polling shifts, diaspora fundraising, and the August 2026 convention process. Track two is Western Area organizing: treat Freetown and its surroundings as the decisive battleground and invest field resources accordingly between now and 2027. Track three is international positioning: sustain Tripartite engagement, deliver on reform commitments visible to ECOWAS and the AU, and build the argument that SLPP is the reform party by demonstration rather than assertion. Track four is internal SLPP cohesion: the surest way for SLPP to lose 2028 is to let its own succession process produce visible division before the APC convention clarifies who SLPP will be running against.

Chapter 10
Annex — Methodology, Sources, Limitations

Evidence Base

This brief draws on: the APC's own public constitution and digital infrastructure; the Institute for Governance Reform's December 2025 national study on flagbearer dynamics; election observer reports from the EU Election Observation Mission and the Carter Center; the Tripartite Committee's recommendations; International IDEA's 2025 democracy assessment; Sierra Leonean and diaspora press coverage from 2023 through May 2026; and on-the-ground reporting around the most recent aspirant declarations. Where evidence is thin, the brief says so rather than inventing certainty.

Limitations

First, the APC field is still open. The August 2026 National Delegates Conference at Bai Bureh Hall in Port Loko will clarify candidacies, and some of the figures profiled here may withdraw, enter, or merge their campaigns before then. Second, polling data is sparse. The IGR December 2025 survey is the strongest evidence available, but a single survey taken eight months before the convention should be treated as a baseline, not a prediction. Third, internal party information is partial. APC's internal bargaining happens informally, and the brief relies on reported outputs rather than direct observation of the negotiation process. Fourth, the brief is deliberately one-sided. It is designed to serve SLPP planning, not to produce a neutral academic assessment.
